Carbon-14

Carbon-14 dating, also called radiocarbon dating, is a method of age determination that depends on the decay of radiocarbon (carbon-14) into nitrogen. Carbon-14 is continuously formed in nature by the interaction of neutrons with nitrogen-14 in the Earth's atmosphere; the neutrons necessary for this reaction are produced by cosmic rays interacting with the atmosphere.

The radiocarbon present in atmospheric carbon dioxide molecules enters the biological carbon cycle: green plants absorb it from the air and then transmit it to animals through the food chain. Radiocarbon decays slowly in a living organism, and the amount lost is continually replenished whenever the organism takes in air or food. However, once the organism dies, it stops absorbing carbon-14 , so the amount of radiocarbon in its tissues decreases steadily. Because carbon-14 decays at this constant rate, an estimate of when an organism died can be made by measuring the amount of its residual radiocarbon.

The carbon-14 method was developed by American physicist Willard F. Libby around 1946. It has proven to be a versatile technique for dating fossils and archaeological specimens from 500 to 50,000 years old. The method is widely used by geologists, anthropologists, archaeologists and Pleistocene researchers in related fields.
