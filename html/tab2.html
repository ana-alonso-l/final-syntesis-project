The parallax method

The parallax method is based on classical geometry and consists of observing the displacements that are produced in the apparent position of the stars as the Earth moves around the Sun.

This method can be appreciated with a simple experiment : place your finger thumb close to your nose and alternately wink your eyes. You will notice that the thumb moves from left to right with respect to the background, if you repeat the operation with the arm extended, you will see that the displacement with respect to the background of the thumb is less. This perspective effect is known as parallax and can also be observed in the stars. As the Earth revolves around the Sun in a year, our position, our "eye", does not have a fixed position. On January 1 and July 1 (six months later) the Earth occupies diametrically opposite positions in its orbit.

On those two dates, a nearby star (A) will be seen at different positions with respect to the backdrop of stars and constellations farther away. By complicated procedures, astronomers have managed to calculate the angle p (called the parallax of that star); p is the angle at which the radius of the Earth's orbit would be seen from that star.

As the distance to the stars is very large, these angles, as shown in the following table, are always very small, in the order of fractions of 1 arcsecond, i.e., very small. Until the telescopes were not powerful enough, it was not possible to measure the parallax of any star. The first time it was achieved was in 1838, when the German astronomer Friedrich Bessel managed to measure the parallax of the star 61 Cygni. But what matters to us is that this angle only depends on the distance, and vice versa: knowing this angle we can calculate the distance.

The usual unit for measuring astronomical distances is the light-year: the distance light travels in one year. To simplify calculations, astronomers have devised a new unit of distance: the parsec (pc). 1 parsec (short for parallax second) is the distance a star has to be so that its parallax angle is p = 1″. The equivalence between the two units is 1 pc = 3.26 light years.
